Yale SA · 02 · ~125 words“What is something about which you have changed your mind in the last three years? What changed it?”

For most of my life I thought my grandmother's Ankara fabric scraps were sentimental — the soft orange-and-cobalt remnants she carried from Lagos in a tin tea box, and the way her hands smelled like cumin when she folded them. I kept them in a drawer and called it heritage.

Then last spring, I started layering them under the oil portraits I was painting for my studio portfolio. The fabric showed through the skin — not as background, but as the thing the face was made of.

That was the first time I understood that family is identity — that whose face I'm painting is also whose cloth I'm painting on, and the two cannot be separated.

I used to think the scraps were a souvenir. I was wrong. They are an argument, and I am still learning to make it.

Yale SA · 02 · ~125 words“What is something about which you have changed your mind in the last three years? What changed it?”

For most of my life I thought my grandmother's Ankara fabric scraps were sentimental — the indigo and saffron remnants she carried from Lagos in a tin tea box, kept in the back of a drawer I rarely opened. I called it heritage and left it there.

Then last spring, I started layering them under the oil portraits I was painting for my studio portfolio. The cloth bled through the skin. The fabric showed through the face — not as background, but as the thing the face was made of.

I had been painting an answer; the cloth made it a question. Whose family is in any given face — and who decides?

I used to think the scraps were a souvenir. They are an argument I am still learning how to make in oil.
